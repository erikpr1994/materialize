This platform is your own technical blog (with syndication to dev.to/Hashnode/Medium); the reader is an engineer triaging ruthlessly on HN, feeds, and search, deciding in one title-plus-paragraph whether your work is real.

- **Open on the concrete problem, result, or surprising claim** — first 1-2 sentences state what broke, what you built, or what you found. No throat-clearing; get "what and why-care" above the first scroll, defer setup below it.
- **Put a specific, honest number or named system in the title** — "cut p99 from 800ms to 90ms" beats "improving latency." On HN/feeds the title is the whole pitch; specificity is credibility and distribution at once, and editorialized hype gets flagged.
- **Show your work with real artifacts** — actual code, config, graphs, screenshots, numbers, exact commands, not paraphrase. Engineers earn trust from things they can reproduce, check, or steal; abstract narration reads as content marketing.
- **Structure for scanners and machines** — descriptive H2/H3 headings (question-form is fine), short paragraphs, a TL;DR near the top. Scanners extract value in 30 seconds and answer engines extract passages to cite.
- **Let depth set the length, never a word count** — deep dives and postmortems run ~1,500-4,000+ words when every section resolves something; a tight 600-word writeup that fully answers one question beats a padded 2,000. The hard rule is zero filler.
- **Own the canonical copy on your domain** — syndicate to dev.to / Hashnode / Medium with `rel=canonical` pointing back. The tag consolidates SEO equity to you; the copies still drive referral traffic. Without it, duplicates split or cannibalize ranking.
- **Give it a deliberate first push** — submit to HN/Reddit/Lobsters with an accurate, non-clickbait title; seed through your network and relevant newsletters. Algorithmic reach is downstream of a credible initial signal, not time-of-day games.
- **Use plain markdown the platform renders** — fenced code blocks with language hints, real hyperlinks, descriptive image alt text. dev.to's ~4 tags are topic classifiers, not reach hacks; skip hashtags and decorative emoji on long-form.
- **Write for one real search/question intent** — place the keyword naturally in title, first paragraph, and a heading or two, then stop. Comprehensively answering the question a developer actually types (or asks an AI) earns ranking and citation; repetition trips spam detection.

## For a project post
- Lead with the shipped result or the constraint that made it hard, not the backstory.
- Walk the actual build: decisions you reversed, the config that bit you, the benchmark that settled an argument — paste the real thing.
- For a postmortem or build log, include a timeline, expected-vs-actual, root cause, and the durable lesson — and be honest about what went wrong; sanitized writeups read as PR and don't spread.
- End on the lesson or the open question, not a CTA.

## Skip
- **Keyword stuffing / density** — now actively detectable; natural placement is sufficient.
- **Posting at the optimal HN time** — marginal at best; an accurate title on genuinely interesting work carries it.
- **Engagement-bait endings** ("drop a comment", "smash that bookmark") — read as social-media import and earn nothing; verifiable work earns the replies.
- **Optimizing for views/impressions/claps** — vanity metrics; judge a post afterward by saves, substantive replies, reproductions, and inbound links, not while writing.
