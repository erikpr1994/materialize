LinkedIn is a professional feed read in passing on mobile; people skim, expand a few posts, and judge your credibility by specifics.

- **Spend the hook on the first 2-3 lines** — only ~140-210 chars show before "…see more", and that fragment alone decides the expand. Lead with a concrete: a number, a result, a named tradeoff ("Cut p99 from 4s to 1.5s — one query did it"), never "Excited to share".
- **Optimize for dwell and substantive comments, not likes** — ranking favors time-spent and real reply threads; likes are cheap and weakly weighted. Write something complete on-platform that invites a genuine answer.
- **Keep the value native to the feed** — off-platform posts get down-distributed (~26-42% lower reach in observational data). Deliver the insight in the post; treat any link as a bonus, not the payload.
- **If you need a link, put it in the first comment — as a hedge, not a law** — body links correlate with lower reach, so a link-free post plus a comment link is a reasonable bet, but the benefit is confounded by selection bias and LinkedIn denies a penalty. Don't over-trust it.
- **Write like an engineer telling a colleague what you built, what broke, what you learned** — real numbers and tradeoffs. Constraints plus a measurable, verifiable outcome is what a technical crowd rewards and remembers.
- **Structure as context → action → measurable outcome, one idea per post** — "redesigned token refresh, cut failed-login retries 40% at peak" lands; "worked on auth" does not.
- **Aim for one idea in ~150-250 words (~800-1,300 chars)** — 800-1,000-char posts perform well; 3,000 is the hard cap. A tight 600-char incident writeup beats a padded essay.
- **Plain text with line breaks, never markdown** — asterisks and backticks render literally; there are no code blocks. 1-2 line paragraphs with blank lines between them for mobile skimmability. Never fake bold with Unicode — it breaks screen readers and reads as spam.
- **Engage genuinely in the first hour** — early velocity is a strong distribution signal and author replies extend dwell. Reply with substance, not "thanks!". It amplifies a good post; it won't rescue a weak one.
- **Prefer native carousels, native video, and real screenshots** — swipe/watch formats get favorable distribution and high dwell; a screenshot of what you shipped grounds the claim. Carousels do best at ~8-12 slides, feed video under ~60-90s with a captioned opening. Weightings drift — treat as directional.
- **Ask a real, specific question only when you want answers** — a targeted technical question draws the substantive comments that build reputation; generic "thoughts?" reads as influencer bait and a technical audience discounts you for it.
- **Use 2-3 topical hashtags max** — they're a weak discovery signal now, not a reach multiplier; more looks spammy. @-mention people only when it's genuinely relevant.

## For a project post
- Open on the outcome or the surprise: the metric you moved, the thing that broke, the tradeoff you chose — not "I built a thing".
- One sentence of context (the constraint), the action, the measurable result. Cut everything not serving that line.
- Let the work be the differentiator: make the GitHub/demo the proof, not the hook. The post creates curiosity; the artifact closes it — and the work must match the claim or it backfires.
- A real screenshot of what you shipped grounds the claim and earns the click; peers rarely dig into your repo unprompted.
- End with a specific question or the one tradeoff you're still unsure about, if you actually want the discussion.

## Skip
- **Unicode bold/italic formatting** — breaks screen readers, looks like spam, helps nothing.
- **Broetry, one. line. at. a. time.** — 2018-era cosplay that reads as influencer to a technical crowd.
- **Walls of hashtags (10-30)** — weak discovery signal, spammy; 2-3 is plenty.
- **Posting at a "magic" time (9:07am Tuesday)** — Tue-Thu business hours is a mild edge at best; a strong post at a mediocre time beats a weak one at the "perfect" time.
- **Posting every single day to grow** — low-signal filler erodes the expert credibility you're building; there's no magic cadence either, signal beats frequency.
- **Engagement pods / comment-for-comment rings** — increasingly detected, hollow threads a technical audience notices, and it trains you to optimize the wrong signal.
- **Optimizing for likes** — the weakest meaningful signal; dwell and real comments dominate.
- **Generic "agree? thoughts?" questions** — draw low-signal replies and cost you credibility.
- **Treating "link in body kills reach 90%" as law** — overstated; the measured hit is ~26-42% and profiles are hit far less than company pages. A tradeoff to weigh, not a ban.
